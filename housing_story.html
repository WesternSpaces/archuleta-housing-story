1 Meet Your Neighbors

These aren't real people, but they represent real situations in our community. As you read, consider: Which household feels most familiar to you?

🏥
Maria: The Healthcare Worker
CNA at Pagosa Springs Medical Center, $42,000/year (~50% AMI)

Maria is 34 and has been here five years. She's great at her job and loves the community. She rents a one-bedroom apartment and works extra shifts when she can. She's looked at buying, but the math doesn't work. She's considered moving to Durango, where some coworkers commute from, but doesn't want to spend two hours a day in a car.

What she can afford: $1,050/month rent | Homeownership out of reach
👪
The Garcias: The Young Family
Deputy sheriff + teacher's aide, two kids, $93,000/year (~80% AMI)

Carlos and Elena are in their late 30s with two kids in elementary school. They've rented the same three-bedroom house for six years. The landlord just told them he's selling—and the new owner may not keep it as a rental. They've started looking to buy, but listings under $400,000 are rare.

What they can afford: $2,325/month rent | ~$350,000 home
🍳
The Rodriguezes: The Restaurant Owners
Downtown restaurant owners for 12 years

Miguel and Teresa aren't looking for housing themselves—they own their home. But they can't find workers. Last summer, they had to close two days a week because they couldn't staff the kitchen. When they do find applicants, many back out once they see rental prices.

"We've turned away business. We've shortened hours. It's not a labor problem—it's a housing problem."

What their employees need: Line cooks ($35k) need $875/mo | Servers ($30k) need $750/mo
🏠
Robert: The Retiring Teacher
25 years at Pagosa Springs Middle School, $32,000/year (~40% AMI)

Robert, 67, retired two years ago and lives on Social Security plus a modest pension. He owns his home, but it's more house than he needs now. He'd like to downsize to something smaller and more accessible—maybe a condo or apartment—and free up equity. But there's nothing like that on the market.

What he could afford: $800/month rent | ~$120,000 home (if he downsized)

2 The Math Doesn't Work

Area Median Income (AMI)

AMI is the middle point of all household incomes in our area—half earn more, half earn less. HUD calculates it yearly.

For Archuleta County in 2025 (2-person household):

  • 100% AMI = $81,600/year
  • 80% AMI = $65,300/year ("low income" in housing programs)
  • 50% AMI = $40,800/year ("very low income")

Housing is "affordable" when you spend no more than 30% of income on it. Spend more, and you're "cost-burdened."

Let's follow each household through the affordability calculation—comparing what they can afford to what housing actually costs.

The jobs-housing imbalance: Pagosa Springs has 3.5 jobs for every working household—one of the most extreme ratios in Colorado. Our economy needs more workers than can live here.

Real Impacts

  • Restaurants close early because they can't staff the kitchen
  • Healthcare facilities limit services because nurses can't find housing
  • Schools struggle to hire teachers and support staff
  • Contractors turn down work because they can't find laborers

When workers can't live here, businesses can't operate here.

4 We're Not Starting From Scratch

Good news: our community has already taken steps to address housing. Here's what exists—and how it connects to our four households.

For Maria (Healthcare Worker, ~50% AMI)

  • Housing Choice Vouchers — Rental assistance (~30 vouchers, program at capacity)
  • Hickory Ridge Apartments — 40 units for ≤60% AMI
  • Rose Mountain Townhomes — 34 new units at 30-60% AMI (opened 2024)

The gap: Waitlists are long. Maria earns slightly too much for some programs but not enough for market rent.

For the Garcias (Working Family, ~80% AMI)

  • HomesFund Down Payment Assistance — Second mortgages of $15k-$50k
  • Pagosa Springs CDC Workforce Housing — Chris Mountain homes ~$350k
  • Habitat for Humanity — Affordable homeownership (1-3 homes/year)

The gap: Few homes exist at their price point. Down payment assistance can't bridge a $200k+ gap.

For the Rodriguezes (Employers)

  • HOWL Program (new 2025) — Employer-linked workforce assistance
  • Pipeline projects — Pagosa Views, Timberline, The Enclave with deed-restricted units

The gap: No employer-assisted housing program exists yet. HOWL is a pilot. Large-scale workforce housing is years away.

For Robert (Senior, ~40% AMI)

  • Casa de los Arcos — 16 senior units at 0-30% AMI
  • Socorro Senior Living — 19 senior units at 0-30% AMI
  • Archuleta Seniors, Inc. — Aging-in-place support services

The gap: Very limited senior housing. Long waitlists. Few options between "stay in your house" and "move away."

What's in the Pipeline

About 1,100 housing units are in various stages of planning:

Good news: If these projects move forward, they could meet much of projected need.

The catch: Most serve moderate- to higher-income households. Fewer address the 30-60% AMI gap.

The Housing Action Plan builds on this foundation. Housing is already a priority in both Town and County strategic plans. The question isn't whether to act—it's how.
